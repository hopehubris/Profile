AI & Automation

We deflected thousands of tickets a month with AI. Here's what we actually built.

Most AI-in-IT-support stories start with the technology. Ours started long before ChatGPT and Claude with a divided team, metrics nobody trusted, and a single mission: get the number of minutes LinkedIn employees lost waiting for IT to zero. The AI came much later than you'd expect — and that's exactly the point.

When I took over LinkedIn's enterprise support operation 2018, we had roughly 22,000 employees and were running 30–35,000 tickets a month covering basic IT support — account management, network, hardware. Seventy percent of break/fix tickets were taking 4.5 days to resolve. By any measure, that was a significant drag on a company where the average fully-loaded employee cost was over $100 an hour.

But the number that troubled me most wasn't the resolution time. It was that we couldn't trust any of our own numbers.

The real problem: nobody trusted the data

Every time our team reported on performance, the conversation filled up with caveats. "Well, it depends on how you count it." "That metric doesn't capture the full picture." The data was being massaged — not maliciously, but because the underlying systems weren't clean and nobody wanted to deliver bad news without a qualifier attached. The result was that we had no reliable baseline. You cannot improve what you cannot honestly measure.

On top of that, we had a structural team problem. Our managed service provider staff and our internal FTEs were operating as separate entities with separate motivations. They didn't understand that they were one team, and that the success of either depended on both. That division was costing us coordination time, accountability, and morale.

So before I touched a single automation or AI tool, I had two prerequisites: unify the team under a shared identity and shared metrics, and make our data an open book that anyone could validate without caveats. Raw numbers, visible to everyone, no interpretation required.

The mission that changed how we thought about everything

Once we had clean data and a unified team, we set a goal that reframed how the entire organization thought about IT support.

IT support at most companies does not generate revenue or increase profit directly. But every minute a salesperson, an engineer, or a finance lead can't do their job because of the tools we provide? It costs the company real money.

At a loaded cost of over $100 per employee per hour, a ticket that takes 4.5 days to resolve isn't just an inconvenience — it's a quantifiable business loss. We set our north star accordingly: zero minutes of lost productivity waiting for IT. Not a faster response time. Zero. That goal sounds impossible, and it is — but it's the right impossible to aim at.

Step one was not automation. It was process.

Clean data showed us something we didn't want to see: we had a lot of broken processes and unnecessarily complex ones. Before we could automate anything, we had to make three explicit decisions about every process in our operation:

  • Fix it — if it was broken but necessary
  • Kill it — if it didn't add value and didn't need to exist
  • Simplify it — if it was more complex than the problem required

One example: app access approvals. We looked at the most commonly distributed applications across the company. Where an app was used by a sufficiently high percentage of an organization, we proposed converting it to be globally provisioned — no request, no ticket, just access on day one. For apps that had approval workflows but were almost never rejected (Acrobat Reader, Figma, tools like that), we changed the approval email to a notification email. The manager found out their employee got access, rather than being asked to approve it. Small change. Significant friction removed from the manager and the employee.

We then built integrations between ServiceNow and our endpoint management systems so that provisioning happened automatically when those conditions were met. No ticket. No wait. No lost first-day productivity for a new employee who couldn't access their tools.

When AI actually entered the picture

Only after the processes were clean and the integrations were working did we introduce AI — first for ticket routing, then as a front-line agent. The routing logic was straightforward but high-impact: if a frontline support staff member couldn't resolve a ticket, having them as the first point of contact was just adding time for the sake of process. We took them out of the flow for those ticket types, routing directly to the person or team that could actually resolve it.

Then the pandemic happened. In 2020, with offices closed and the entire company working remotely, a team I led launched LAMABot — LinkedIn's Ask Me Anything Bot, built on Moveworks — as our always-on support agent. When employees were asking questions in Slack or on Teams, LAMABot could surface answers from our knowledge base and any other information we'd fed it, immediately, at any hour. The timing was fortunate: the forced shift to remote work made the case for self-service that we'd been struggling to make in person.

The results

By the time our numbers peaked, we had grown to nearly 30,000 employees and significantly expanded our scope — from basic IT support to also covering Salesforce, Dynamics, Finance systems, HR Tech, and InfoSec. More employees, more services, same MSP contract cost.

The hardest sell — and the lesson we learned the hard way

The biggest internal battle wasn't with a vendor or a system. It was with the belief — held by my own team, our customers, and leadership — that in-person, white-glove support was the gold standard. As offices reopened in 2022 and 2023, I proposed reducing walk-up support locations from 15+ down to fewer than 5, replacing them with LAMABot, ServiceNow self-service, and scheduled interactions only. I wanted to redirect the savings into tooling and more advanced engineering talent.

I was overruled. The instinct toward in-person support was too strong. At the time, it was a 50/50 bet on which would give us the higher returns. The irony: cost-cutting over the past two years has eliminated those support staff anyway — without the automation investment that would have made the transition work well. The locations are losing their people with no self-service infrastructure to absorb the load. Being right doesn't feel good when it happens the wrong way.

The second lesson was about engineering alignment. The most underrated obstacle to IT support transformation at most enterprises isn't tools or budget — it's getting product engineering teams to treat existing stability as equally important to shipping new things. Every broken product or environment means a ticket gets created. My team's goal was zero tickets. Engineering's incentive was new features. Those motivations weren't aligned, and we never fully solved it.

What I'd do differently with today's tools

If I were starting this today, four things would change:

  • Give AI tools broad access to ingest information from across the environment from day one — so they can act as front-line support immediately, not after months of knowledge base construction
  • Use that breadth proactively to identify inefficient processes and policies before they generate tickets
  • Make every team responsible for providing the self-service and monitoring inputs that the AI needs — support tooling is everyone's problem, not just IT's
  • Require all interactions to run through self-service tooling first, with human escalation as the exception rather than the default

The thing most companies get wrong

AI tools are not capable of doing this alone. They synthesize the inputs you provide into actions and interactions. If your documentation is inconsistent, your processes are undocumented, and your data isn't trusted — the AI will automate your chaos faster than you can clean it up.

The second failure mode is access. There is a real balance to strike between privacy, security, and utility. At LinkedIn, we leaned heavily into privacy and security controls, which meant our tools couldn't do all they were capable of. That was an own-goal. The constraints were understandable, but we never fully reckoned with the cost of limiting the tools we were paying to use.

The companies that get this right aren't the ones with the most sophisticated AI. They're the ones that did the unglamorous work first — trusted data, unified teams, clean processes — and treated the AI as the last step of the transformation rather than the first.
